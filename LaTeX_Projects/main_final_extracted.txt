                                Abstract


   This report presents the design and development of the PRO-WISE
PLATFORM, a QR-based product assistance system aimed at improv-
ing access to product information and support services.


   The system enables users to scan QR codes to quickly identify prod-
ucts, retrieve relevant information, and obtain assistance for common
issues.


   The platform is built using modern web and mobile technologies, en-
suring accessibility, usability, and scalability.


   This solution enhances user experience by simplifying the assistance
process and reducing response time.


   Keywords: QR Code, Product Assistance, Web Application, Mobile

General Introduction

In the contemporary digital era, the rapid proliferation of software-enabled
consumer goods and complex industrial systems has significantly ele-
vated user expectations regarding product support and service delivery.
While modern consumers expect immediate, intuitive, and highly con-
textual assistance, traditional support mechanisms have largely failed
to adapt. Technical documentation remains predominantly static—often
confined to verbose physical manuals or scattered PDF files—leaving
users to navigate through extensive pages of irrelevant information to
resolve simple operational issues.
  From an organizational standpoint, enterprise manufacturers and ser-
vice providers face a critical dual challenge: maintaining high-quality
customer assistance while scaling support operations and curbing oper-
ational overhead. Conventional channels, including telephone support
hotlines, email ticketing systems, and generalized FAQ sections, are
often unable to address specific, real-time consumer questions. This
operational disconnect leads to prolonged resolution times, increases
the support workload on technical teams, and ultimately diminishes cus-
tomer loyalty.
  To address these challenges, this engineering project introduces PRO-
WISE, an intelligent, QR-code-enabled product assistance platform de-
signed to bridge the gap between physical hardware and digital support
services. Engineered as a multi-tenant, cloud-based ecosystem, PRO-
WISE facilitates direct interaction with digital troubleshooting guides and
localized conversational AI models. By scanning a unique QR code at-
tached to a physical product, users are instantly directed to a context-
aware workspace containing model-specific documentation, interactive

multimedia content, and an AI-powered conversational repair assistant.
This system aims to streamline self-service support, reduce reliance on
human agents, and enhance customer satisfaction through instant, on-
demand troubleshooting.
   This thesis document is structured into seven chapters outlining the
engineering lifecycle of the PRO-WISE platform. The first chapter estab-
lishes the project context, introduces the host organization, and presents
a comparative analysis of existing support paradigms. The second chap-
ter defines the architectural foundations, system requirements, and the
technical development environment. Chapters three through seven trace
the iterative development of the platform across five distinct software re-
leases, covering authentication, digital catalog management, user feed-
back loops, hybrid intelligence with semantic search, and end-to-end
technician maintenance workflows. Finally, a general conclusion sum-
marizes the project’s contributions and suggests future avenues for re-
search and technical development.

1.3     Preliminary Study

1.3.1   Industrial Context

In the modern industrial landscape, digital transformation has fundamen-
tally reshaped user interactions with consumer electronics and complex
machinery. Consumers expect immediate, digital-first support for the
physical products they purchase. Despite this demand, the delivery of
product assistance remains heavily fragmented. Technical documenta-
tion is often stored in scattered PDFs, static websites, or physical man-


                                                                       2
Chapter 1. General Presentation of the Project


uals, making it difficult for users to find the exact help they need when
they need it.

1.3.2   Problem Statement

Traditional product support paradigms impose significant operational chal-
lenges on both consumers and manufacturing organizations. These pain
points are summarized as follows:

   • Severe Information Fragmentation: Critical resources such as
     user guides, video tutorials, and FAQs are scattered across mul-
     tiple, unintegrated media channels, which disrupts the continuity of
     the support process.

   • High Cognitive Load: Printed user guides are frequently exhaus-
     tive, overly technical, and difficult to navigate, which discourages
     users from attempting self-troubleshooting and leads to premature
     customer support escalations.

   • Support Channel Bottlenecks: The absence of localized self-service
     tools forces consumers to rely on phone support, email tickets, or
     on-site technician visits for simple maintenance issues, which in-
     creases response times and drives up operational costs.

   • Absence of Context-Awareness: General online help centers do
     not automatically detect the specific product model, hardware revi-
     sion, or user profile, necessitating manual identification steps that
     are prone to confusion.

Comparative Evaluation and Positioning

The comparative analysis presented in Table 1.1 highlights a distinct gap
in the product support market. While centralized community platforms
like iFixit offer valuable repair data, they lack the organizational partition-
ing and formal content approval structures needed by commercial en-
terprises. Conversely, brand-specific portals like GE Appliances deliver
high-quality, controlled documentation but operate as closed, proprietary
systems that cannot scale to accommodate external manufacturers.

1.4     Adopted Software Development Methodology

1.4.1   Methodological Rationale

Building a multi-tenant platform featuring web interfaces, mobile clients,
and an embedded artificial intelligence layer involves significant archi-
tectural complexity. To handle shifting technical requirements and de-
liver early, functional prototypes, we selected an Agile development ap-
proach. Agile methodologies prioritize iterative development, continuous
validation, and flexible planning over rigid, sequential structures. This al-
lowed us to incrementally implement and refine the platform based on
feedback and integration tests.

1.4.2   The Scrum Framework

Within the Agile paradigm, we implemented the Scrum framework. Scrum
is a lightweight project management framework that organizes software
development into fixed-duration iterations called Sprints (typically lasting
two weeks). Each Sprint focuses on converting a set of prioritized user
requirements from a product backlog into a functional, tested software

                                                                           8
Chapter 1. General Presentation of the Project


increment. For the PRO-WISE project, the development lifecycle was
divided into five successive Sprints, each culminating in a deployable
system release.

1.4.3   Project Roles and Mapping

To adapt the Scrum framework to the context of this academic graduation
project (PFE), the Scrum roles were distributed as follows:
   • Product Owner: This role was shared by the project supervisors

     tablished the business requirements, and prioritized the features of
     the Product Backlog to align with market needs.

   • Scrum Master: Facilitated regular review meetings, ensured com-
     pliance with Agile guidelines, and assisted in resolving technical
     bottlenecks.

   • Development Team: Fulfilled by the student developer, who was

     of the platform.

2.2     Requirements Capture

2.2.1   Actors Identification

The PRO-WISE platform involves the following primary actors, each with
specific interactions within the ecosystem:

   • Client (Customer): The primary consumer who scans QR codes
      to access product information, consults manuals (PDFs) and sup-
      port videos, performs semantic searches for troubleshooting, and
      provides feedback/ratings.

   • Technician: The field expert who creates structured repair guides,
      uploads media assets from the field, and uses AI-assisted drafting
      to share technical knowledge.



                                      13
Chapter 2. Architecture, Tools and Development Environment


   • Company Administrator: Manages the company-specific product
     catalog and team. They are responsible for reviewing and approving
     content submissions before they become visible to clients.

   • Super Administrator: The global platform manager who handles
     company registrations, manages system-wide categories, and en-
     sures platform integrity through audit logs.

   • AI System: An autonomous actor that provides hybrid semantic un-
     derstanding (combining vector embeddings and deterministic match-
     ing), automated content approval scoring based on a dual-layer val-
     idation pipeline (Rules + LLM), and real-time translation of technical
     instructions.

2.2.2   Functional Requirements

The functional requirements of the PRO-WISE platform are divided into
several key modules, designed to ensure data consistency and multi-
tenant partitioning:

   • User Management and Authentication: Multi-role access con-
     trol (Client, Technician, Company Admin, Super Admin) secured by
     JWT authentication and OAuth protocols, with strict tenant isolation.

   • Multi-Tenant Catalog Management: Enabling Company Admins
     to manage brand-specific catalogs (categories, products, manuals,
     and metadata).

   • Structured Knowledge base: Allowing Technicians and Admins to
     draft and edit step-by-step troubleshooting guides, separate from
     raw document files.

   • AI-Powered Support Services: Leveraging the Google Gemini API
     to enable semantic search on technical documentation, generate

                                                                        14
Chapter 2. Architecture, Tools and Development Environment


     recommendation lists, and automate translation.

   • Maintenance and Service Dispatch: A complete workflow en-
     abling Clients to request technical help and Company Admins to
     assign tickets to Technicians.

   • QR-Based Access System: Generating unique, product-linked QR
     codes to route clients directly to model-specific help sections.

2.2.3   Non-Functional Requirements

To guarantee system stability, user satisfaction, and industrial readiness,
the platform satisfies the following technical constraints:

   • Security and Confidentiality: Sensitive data must be encrypted;
     password hashing must leverage bcrypt, and all API endpoints must
     validate user roles and enforce tenant boundaries.

   • Scalability and Performance: The backend REST API must sep-
     arate read/write operations and paginate heavy data sets. Media
     files are offloaded to optimize network transit, ensuring page load
     times under 2 seconds.

   • Usability and Accessibility: The web and mobile user interfaces
     must follow responsive design patterns, ensuring that field techni-
     cians can comfortably access the system on mobile screens.

   • Availability: The public product lookup and QR scanning interfaces
     must be highly available, while administrative dashboards require
     active authorization checks.

   • Maintainability: The codebases must follow standard modular de-
     velopment rules: clear separation of controllers, services, models
     on the backend, and Redux slices and reusable components on the
     frontend.

6.3      Comparative Study of AI Models

To integrate intelligent troubleshooting and recommendation services,
we evaluated different vector embedding and generative models.

6.3.1     Selection Criteria

The selection of the AI models was based on three key criteria:

   • Spatial Accuracy: The vector space must have high dimensions to
      capture fine differences between technical hardware specs.

   • Language Support: The ability to parse technical terms in English,
      French, and Arabic query terms.

   • Infrastructure Overhead: Minimizing local server costs (RAM, GPU)
      by choosing managed cloud APIs over heavy local setups.

6.3.2     Evaluated Models

We compared two main model approaches during our system design:

                                                                                         65
Chapter 6. Release 4 : Hybrid Intelligence Layer, Semantic Search & AI Moderation


  1. BAAI/bge-small-en: A local embedding model that outputs 384-
      dimensional vectors. While lightweight for basic local testing, it only
      supports English and has lower spatial precision.

  2. Google Gemini API: A cloud-based API model providing 768/1536-
      dimensional embeddings (text-embedding-004) and generative an-
      swers (gemini-1.5-flash). It supports multilingual inputs, requires
      zero local compute resources, and provides high accuracy for hard-
      ware manuals.

Cost and Performance Analysis

To justify our choice mathematically, we calculated the monthly running
costs for both systems. We assume a baseline of 5,000 active trou-
bleshooting queries per month.
   For the cloud-based **Google Gemini API** (using gemini-1.5-flash
and text-embedding-004):

   • Average input tokens per request: 1,200 tokens (document context
     + query).

   • Average output tokens per request: 300 tokens (step-by-step reso-

                                                                                67
Chapter 6. Release 4 : Hybrid Intelligence Layer, Semantic Search & AI Moderation


     lution).

   • Average embedding tokens per request: 50 tokens (user search
     text).

Let N = 5, 000 requests. The total token consumption and costs are
calculated as follows:

                      Input Cost = 5, 000 × 1, 200 tokens
                                  = 6M tokens × $0.075/M = $0.45             (6.1)
                    Output Cost = 5, 000 × 300 tokens
                                  = 1.5M tokens × $0.30/M = $0.45            (6.2)
                Embedding Cost = 5, 000 × 50 tokens
                                  = 0.25M tokens × $0.025/M = $0.00625

6.4      RAG Architecture and System Integration

6.4.1    Semantic Search & RAG-Based Troubleshooting

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that combines
information retrieval with text generation. When a user submits a nat-
ural language troubleshooting query, the Sails.js backend fetches rele-
vant guide steps from MongoDB based on vector similarity, and forwards
these steps as context to the Gemini model to write a clear, personalized
reply.

6.4.2    Architecture Without RAG (Keyword-Based Approach)

In the traditional keyword-based approach, the user submits a text query
through the client application. The backend receives this query and
performs a simple string matching operation (SQL LIKE or MongoDB
$regex) against product titles and descriptions stored in the database.
The system returns a flat list of matching documents without any under-
standing of user intent. Figure 6.2 illustrates this simple data flow.




                                                                                69
Chapter 6. Release 4 : Hybrid Intelligence Layer, Semantic Search & AI Moderation




  Figure 6.2: System architecture without RAG – Keyword-based search approach


   This approach is fast but limited. It fails when users use synonyms,
abbreviations, or descriptions that do not match the exact stored terms.
For example, searching for “screen issue” returns no results when the
manual uses “display failure”.

6.4.3   Architecture With RAG (Semantic Search Approach)

The RAG-based architecture replaces keyword matching with a two-
phase intelligence pipeline. In the indexing phase, technical manuals
are split into small text chunks, each chunk is converted into a 768-
dimensional vector via the Gemini Embedding API, and the vectors are

                                                                                70
Chapter 6. Release 4 : Hybrid Intelligence Layer, Semantic Search & AI Moderation


stored alongside the text in MongoDB. In the query phase, the user’s
natural language question is also embedded into a vector, matched against
stored chunks using cosine similarity, and the top matching chunks are
forwarded as context to the Gemini generative model, which produces a
step-by-step troubleshooting answer. Figure 6.3 illustrates this complete
pipeline.




Figure 6.3: System architecture with RAG – Semantic search and generation pipeline


6.4.4   Hybrid Compatibility & Parts Recommendation

The parts recommendation system uses a hybrid engine to recommend
spare components when physical equipment fails. To prevent recom-

                                                                                71
Chapter 6. Release 4 : Hybrid Intelligence Layer, Semantic Search & AI Moderation


mending incompatible components, the system integrates a two-stage
hybrid decision pipeline that combines semantic AI intelligence with strict
deterministic engineering checks. Figure 6.4 details this recommenda-
tion pipeline.




Figure 6.4: Hybrid compatibility and parts recommendation decision pipeline workflow


   The workflow follows these key execution stages:

  1. Initial Extraction: The platform parses the troubleshooting ticket
     or the natural language query submitted by the client to identify the
     affected system component and the equipment model.

  2. Stage 1: Semantic Match Filtering: Candidate spare parts are re-
     trieved from the MongoDB inventory catalog. The platform calls the

                                                                                 72
Chapter 6. Release 4 : Hybrid Intelligence Layer, Semantic Search & AI Moderation


     Gemini Embedding API to represent descriptions as 768-D vectors
     and computes their cosine similarity relative to the query vector.
     Parts scoring below the 0.70 similarity threshold are immediately
     discarded as irrelevant.

  3. Stage 2: Deterministic Rule Check: Parts that pass semantic
     matching are evaluated against three physical hard constraints:

         • Brand Matching: Ensures manufacturer compatibility standards.
         • Socket Compatibility: Verifies physical connectors align (e.g.,
           pins, dimensions).
         • Interface Type Validation: Checks protocol compatibility (e.g.,
           voltage, signaling types).

     If any technical checks fail, the component is categorized as incom-
     patible and filtered out.

  4. Ranked Outputs: Verified parts are sorted based on their seman-
     tic score and presented to the client as certified matching replace-
     ments, preventing human ordering errors.

// Sails.js GeminiService.js helper function
async function generateEmbedding(text) {
    const model = genAI.getGenerativeModel({
      model: "text-embedding-004"
    });
    const result = await model.embedContent(text);
    return result.embedding.values; // 768-D vector
}

6.5.8   Technical Challenges and Solutions

Integrating a cloud-based AI service introduced several design chal-
lenges:

   • API Rate Limits: Google Gemini API imposes rate limits on ac-
      tive calls. Solution: Implemented an in-memory request queue with
      exponential backoff on retry operations.

   • Token Consumption: Long pages consume excessive context to-
      kens. Solution: Manuals are split into small paragraphs (maximum
      1000 characters) before embedding, reducing token usage.
